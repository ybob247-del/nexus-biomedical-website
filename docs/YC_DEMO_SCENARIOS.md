## Demo Scenario 1: PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome)

### Patient Profile

**Demographics:**
- Age: 28
- Biological Sex: Female
- Height: 5'4"
- Weight: 165 lbs (BMI: 28.3 - overweight)

**Symptoms (Select 8):**
- Irregular menstrual cycles
- Difficulty losing weight
- Fatigue or low energy
- Mood swings or irritability
- Acne or skin issues
- Hair thinning or loss (on head)
- Excess body or facial hair
- Brain fog or difficulty concentrating

**Symptom Duration:** More than 12 months

**Lifestyle Factors:**
- Diet Quality: 5/10 (moderate)
- Exercise Frequency: 2 times per week
- Sleep Quality: 6/10
- Stress Level: 6/10

**EDC Exposure:**
- Plastic Use: Frequently use plastic containers for food storage
- Food Sources: Mostly conventional (non-organic)
- Water Source: Tap water (unfiltered)
- Occupational Exposure: Office environment

**Health History:**
- Previous Diagnoses: None
- Current Medications: None
- Family History: Type 2 diabetes (mother)

### Expected Results

**Risk Score:** 65-75 (High Risk)

**Expected AI Clinical Reasoning:**

The AI should identify the classic PCOS symptom pattern and explain the connection between symptoms:

> "The constellation of symptoms including irregular menstrual cycles, difficulty losing weight, excess body/facial hair (hirsutism), acne, and hair thinning strongly suggests polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). PCOS is characterized by hormonal imbalances affecting reproductive hormones (elevated androgens, irregular ovulation) and metabolic function (insulin resistance). The family history of type 2 diabetes further supports this assessment, as PCOS and diabetes share common metabolic pathways. The combination of these symptoms, particularly with BMI in the overweight range, warrants comprehensive hormonal evaluation including testosterone, DHEA-S, LH/FSH ratio, and fasting insulin."

**Expected Hormone Systems Identified:**
- Reproductive (primary)
- Metabolic (secondary)
- Cortisol (tertiary - related to stress)

**Expected Confidence Score:** 75-85%

**Expected Personalized Recommendations:**

The AI should generate recommendations focused on insulin sensitivity, androgen reduction, and cycle regulation:

1. **Dietary Intervention:** "Adopt a low-glycemic diet focusing on whole foods, lean proteins, and high-fiber vegetables to improve insulin sensitivity. Limit refined carbohydrates and sugars, which can exacerbate insulin resistance and androgen production in PCOS."

2. **Exercise Protocol:** "Implement a combination of resistance training (3x/week) and moderate cardio (2-3x/week) to improve insulin sensitivity and support weight management. Research shows that regular exercise can reduce insulin resistance by 20-30% in PCOS patients."

3. **Stress Management:** "Practice daily stress reduction techniques such as meditation or yoga, as chronic stress can worsen hormonal imbalances in PCOS by elevating cortisol and disrupting reproductive hormone regulation."

4. **Sleep Optimization:** "Prioritize 7-8 hours of quality sleep per night, as poor sleep worsens insulin resistance and increases androgen production in PCOS."

**Expected Supplement Recommendations:**

1. **Inositol (Myo-inositol + D-chiro-inositol):** "2000-4000 mg daily in a 40:1 ratio. Research shows inositol improves insulin sensitivity, reduces androgens, and supports menstrual regularity in PCOS. Take with meals for better absorption."

2. **Vitamin D3:** "2000-4000 IU daily. Deficiency is common in PCOS and supplementation improves insulin sensitivity and reproductive function."

3. **Omega-3 Fatty Acids:** "1000-2000 mg EPA/DHA daily. Reduces inflammation and supports metabolic health in PCOS."

4. **Magnesium:** "300-400 mg daily. Supports insulin sensitivity and may help regulate menstrual cycles."

**Expected Test Recommendations:**
- Total Testosterone, Free Testosterone
- DHEA-S (androgen marker)
- LH/FSH ratio (typically elevated in PCOS)
- Fasting Insulin, Fasting Glucose, HbA1c (metabolic markers)
- TSH, Free T4 (rule out thyroid dysfunction)
- Lipid Panel (PCOS increases cardiovascular risk)

### Why This Demo Works

This scenario demonstrates EndoGuard's ability to recognize a complex hormonal syndrome with multiple interconnected symptoms. PCOS is one of the most common hormone disorders (affecting 10% of women of reproductive age), so YC partners may be familiar with it. The AI's ability to connect reproductive symptoms with metabolic dysfunction and family history showcases sophisticated pattern recognition.

- Stress Level: 6/10

**EDC Exposure:**

- Food Sources: Mix of organic and conventional
- Water Source: Filtered tap water
- Occupational Exposure: Office environment

**Health History:**
- Previous Diagnoses: None
- Current Medications: None
- Family History: Thyroid disease (mother - hypothyroidism)

### Expected Results

**Risk Score:** 60-70 (High Risk)

**Expected AI Clinical Reasoning:**

The AI should recognize the classic hypothyroid symptom pattern:

> "The symptom pattern of fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain resistance, dry skin, hair thinning, brain fog, and constipation is highly characteristic of hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid). These symptoms reflect the systemic effects of reduced thyroid hormone production, which slows metabolism, reduces body temperature regulation, and impairs cognitive function. The family history of maternal hypothyroidism significantly increases risk, as thyroid disorders have a strong genetic component. The constellation of symptoms, particularly with poor sleep quality (which can both cause and result from thyroid dysfunction), warrants comprehensive thyroid function testing including TSH, Free T4, Free T3, and thyroid antibodies (TPO, TG) to assess for autoimmune thyroiditis (Hashimoto's disease)."

**Expected Hormone Systems Identified:**
- Thyroid (primary)
- Metabolic (secondary - affected by thyroid function)

**Expected Confidence Score:** 80-90%

**Expected Personalized Recommendations:**

1. **Nutrient Support:** "Ensure adequate intake of thyroid-supportive nutrients including iodine (from seafood, seaweed, iodized salt), selenium (from Brazil nuts, fish, eggs), and zinc (from meat, shellfish, legumes). These minerals are essential for thyroid hormone production and conversion."

2. **Sleep Hygiene:** "Prioritize improving sleep quality to 7-8 hours per night with consistent sleep/wake times. Poor sleep can worsen thyroid function and exacerbate fatigue. Create a cool, dark sleep environment and avoid screens 1-2 hours before bed."

3. **Gentle Exercise:** "Maintain moderate exercise (walking, swimming, yoga) 3-4 times per week, but avoid excessive high-intensity training which can further stress an underactive thyroid. Exercise supports metabolism and energy levels."

4. **Stress Management:** "Implement daily stress reduction practices, as chronic stress can suppress thyroid function through the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid axis. Consider meditation, deep breathing, or gentle yoga."

**Expected Supplement Recommendations:**

1. **Selenium:** "200 mcg daily. Selenium is essential for converting T4 to active T3 and may reduce thyroid antibodies in autoimmune thyroid disease. Do not exceed 400 mcg daily."

2. **Vitamin D3:** "2000-4000 IU daily. Deficiency is common in hypothyroidism and supplementation may improve thyroid function and reduce autoimmune activity."

3. **Zinc:** "15-30 mg daily. Required for thyroid hormone production and T4 to T3 conversion. Take with food to avoid nausea."

4. **Omega-3 Fatty Acids:** "1000-2000 mg EPA/DHA daily. Reduces inflammation and supports thyroid health, particularly in autoimmune thyroid disease."

**Expected Test Recommendations:**
- TSH (primary screening test)
- Free T4, Free T3 (active thyroid hormones)
- Thyroid Peroxidase Antibodies (TPO) - screens for Hashimoto's
- Thyroglobulin Antibodies (TG) - screens for autoimmune thyroid disease
- Vitamin D, B12 (commonly deficient in hypothyroidism)

### Why This Demo Works

Hypothyroidism is extremely common (affects 5% of the US population, mostly women) and has a clear symptom pattern that the AI can easily recognize. The family history component demonstrates how EndoGuard incorporates genetic risk factors. This scenario also shows how the AI distinguishes between different hormone systemsâ€”thyroid versus reproductive or cortisol.

### Expected Results

**Risk Score:** 70-80 (High Risk)

**Expected AI Clinical Reasoning:**

The AI should recognize the low testosterone pattern and metabolic connections:

> "The symptom constellation of fatigue, decreased muscle mass, increased body fat, decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, mood changes, and brain fog in a 38-year-old male with BMI in the overweight range and sedentary lifestyle strongly suggests hypogonadism (low testosterone). **Expected Hormone Systems Identified:**
- Reproductive (primary - testosterone deficiency)

- Cortisol (tertiary - high stress)

2. **Dietary Overhaul:** "Adopt a whole-foods diet rich in healthy fats (avocados, nuts, olive oil, fatty fish), lean proteins, and vegetables. Reduce processed foods and refined carbohydrates to improve insulin sensitivity. Adequate dietary fat (30-35% of calories) is essential for testosterone production."

3. **Sleep Optimization:** "Prioritize 7-9 hours of quality sleep per night. Testosterone production peaks during sleep, and sleep deprivation can reduce testosterone by 10-15%. Maintain consistent sleep/wake times and create a dark, cool sleep environment."

5. **EDC Reduction:** "Immediately replace plastic food containers and water bottles with glass or stainless steel alternatives. Avoid microwaving food in plastic. Choose organic produce when possible, especially for high-pesticide foods (strawberries, spinach, apples). These steps can significantly reduce endocrine disruptor exposure."

**Expected Supplement Recommendations:**

1. **Vitamin D3:** "3000-5000 IU daily. Vitamin D acts as a steroid hormone precursor and deficiency is strongly associated with low testosterone. Aim for blood levels of 40-60 ng/mL."

2. **Zinc:** "25-30 mg daily. Zinc is essential for testosterone production and deficiency is common in men with low testosterone. Take with food to avoid nausea."

3. **Magnesium:** "400-500 mg daily. Supports testosterone production, improves sleep quality, and enhances insulin sensitivity. Take before bed."

4. **Omega-3 Fatty Acids:** "2000-3000 mg EPA/DHA daily. Reduces inflammation, supports cardiovascular health, and may improve testosterone levels."

**Expected Test Recommendations:**
- Total Testosterone (ideally measured in morning when highest)

- LH, FSH (distinguish primary vs. secondary hypogonadism)
